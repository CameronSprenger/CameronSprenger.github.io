Tilt-Translation Sled (TTS)

The Tilt-Translation Sled is a device donated by the Neuroscience Laboratory of NASA Johnson Space Center. It is capable of administering motions in the roll tilt and X-axis translation. The goal of the experiment is to measure spatial awareness relying solely on their vestibular senses over the duration of a motion profile. Motion stimuli can be administered in a variety of profiles (i.e. sinusoidal, sigmoidal, etc.) and at various frequencies.

Center Image


I joined this project a few years into its lifetime so my role was focused on development and maintainance of the sub-systems. In an effort to expand the testing capabilities of the TTS, I was in charge of the addition of a modular input device that collects user data during the duration of the experiment. There were preexisting 1/2" threaded holes that supported all-thread over the subject head. I used these as a structural base for a tablet running test software the subject interacts with. I was only given a few constraints and considerations to work within, so I had to create a design from sctratch. One of my main focuses when I was in the early stages of ideating was the work flow and ease of use. The point of the device was to be quickly changed back and forth between experiments so I had to consider rapid mounting solutions.

Center Image


A few key design features of the modular mounted device are the magnetic closure latches, the replaceable mounting stand-offs, and the bump stop hinge. Each feature increases work flow or reduces maintainance time if a part were to break. The ieda behind the magnetic latch closure was a solution to quickly access the inside of the housing while still holding the tablet tightly inside. To open the case, the latches are easily lifted up and out of the way, while still maintaining a strong enough closure force to hold the tablet in. The bump stop hinges provided a physical end to the range of motion of the housing so it would not interfere as the operator changed devices between experiments. The largest design feature that the device housing had was the replaceable mounting stand offs. Since the housing was 3-D printed and quite large, if something were to happen to the case, a new part could be printed off individually, saving many hours of manufacturing.

During my time working with the lab, the singe largest hiccup the TTS experienced was the shearing of the mounting bolts that fixed the air bearings to the main chassis. The specifications for the flatness of the operating track are a delta of 0.0002‚Äù per foot, yet our track was far from this which resulted in immense shearing forces as the bearing moved across the track. On a few occasions the suport bolt would shear during a run leaving the air bearing unsupported and free to impact other components of the TTS.

Center Image

I worked with a couple grad students to design a better support system for the air bearing to treat the symptom of the irregular floor. Initially I performed an analysis on the motion and forces on the bolt to determine what kind of load the new support structure would need to withstand. This fix was meant to be temporary while we addressed the issue of the floor flatness. To quantify the flatness of the track, I made a fixture that held a precise level and used thin metal shims to find the difference in height between points on the track. We marked out high and low points in the track surface and treated the areas accordingly through careful sanding.

Vestibular Immobilizer- Roto Chair

Another set of experiments the lab was running was spatial awareness test in the vertical (yaw) axis. This experiment was run on a VR chair capable of programmable rotational profiles. The experiment was also centered around isolating the vestibular system, so the test subject's head had to be fixed in a known and repeatable location for the data to be valid. I was in charge of designing a system that would integrate with the existing mounts in the VR chair, and comfortably support the subject's head during the experiment.

Before I began designing anything, I needed to define the key performance indicators by defining quantifiable metrics for a successful immobilizer. The primary function of the vestibular immobilizer is to restrict motion of the subject's head relative to the roto chair. The two axis that the head is least supported in are the up/down (yaw) and left/right (pitch) axis, so the immobilized needed to provide support for those axis primarily. The secondary function of the immobilizer is to integrate with an HTC vive headset that displays various vision profiles designed by the experimentor. And, the tertiary function of the immobilizer is to be quickly set up for reduced time between experiments.

For the primary function of immobilization, I drew inspiration from the mechanism of another device in the lab used for a similar application. In the TTS there is an effective head immobilizer that restricts motion but moving two padded plates in and out on rails and a threaded center rod. So, I sketched up a similar design and dimensioned it based off of average head geometry and the Vive.

Center Image



The design I came up with functions a such. A central rod runs the length of the device and is oppositely threaded on each end, right-handed on the right, and left-handed on the left. This moves two blocks in and out to which the cheeck pads are fixed. The sliding blocks sit on four rails that prevent the blocks from simply rotation, and direct the force from the central rod to move the blocks in and out. The central rod is operated by a lever that both the test subject and operator can access. Manufacturing took about two weeks because of my limited machine shop experience at the time. I used a drill press and lathe to make most of the components, but a mill would have reduced the manufacturing time by a factor of 4. The first set of pads I designed were dimensioned around the the Vive and sat in a single plane. Much to my disappointment, the first pad design was quite frankly terrible. They weren't comfortable feeling and they did very little to prevent forward pitch of the head.

Center Image


I went back to the drawing board and restarted the design process for the pads. I changed the design theory that the pads would only make contact with the subject's face, and instead used the built-in ear cups of the Vive to add another point of contact. I took new measurements, and added adjustability to fit a broader range of users.

The final iteration was very successful at supporting the test subject's head and provided a strong basis for the experiments the lab wanted to run.

Human Eccentric Rotator Device (HERD)

The final project I worked on for the CU Bioastronautics department was updating the HERD to run a broader range of experiments. HERD is a centrifuge used to induce centripetal acceleration creating atrificial gravity in two orientations. Before getting updated, the HERD had a single configuration with a horizontal bed that induced artificial gravity >1G in the vertical axis of the subject. I worked with two graduate students to add an additional configuration that placed the test subject in a vertical orientation. An entirely new superstructure was designed that held a chair approximately 6"-8" from the center and created artificial gravity normal to the subjects torso.
