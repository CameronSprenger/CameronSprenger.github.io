Headphone Amplifier

The Project and Inspiration

As part of a graduate product design course, I was tasked with designing something that was aesthetically beautiful regardless of functionality. I decided on creating a headphone amplifier that used the Swiss style of watchmaking known as côtes de Genève, or Geneva Stripes to blend my Swiss hetitage and my passion for audio gear into something beautiful I could enjoy for many many years. And yes, it is very functional.

High end audio, past a certain point, is all about the grandeur and presence in a room. When spending upwards of ten of thousand dollars on something, you want it to be beautiful, and you want it to be seen. So, I thought drawing inspiration from Swiss watches would blend together nicely and compliment one another. Since I'd always appreciated the intricacy and complexity of mechanical Swiss watches, I felt it was a nice way to tie the two together and add a high end element to the headphone amplifier.

Design

A major part of the design process was developing the layout of the front panel as well as the back I/O plate. At a minimum, the front faceplate needed to have a volume knob, input selector, power button, and output audio jack. Since there were going to be two control knobs, I decided to have a symmetrical layout for the volume and input on the front panel to have a clear line of seperation between the two, as to make confusing them harder. I placed the knobs along the center line of the faceplate since there wasn't much space vertically to play with and moved the position around horizontally until I felt it had a balanced look to it. I had a few designs for the positions of the output audio jack and power button that fit around the positioned knobs. First, I designed the faceplate to have the power button in the middle, and the output on the left or right side, but, after thinking about the user experience decided to change the design.

The final layout and design I decided on was having the output audio jack in the middle of the faceplate slightly below the vertical centerline to break apart the linearity of the design and change the weighting of the look. This placed the power button off to the viewers left in a natural way that followed the natural instinct to view from left to right. Center Image


A small feature you may have noticed in the model above is the 4 flat head screws holding the faceplate to the main chassis of the amplifier. I chose to use these because in Swiss watches, tiny flat head screws are used to join the internal components, so it is yet another element I drew inspiration from in the design of the amplifier. After a bit of diggin in some parts bins, I found 4 flat head shoulder bolts that were the correct size I was looking for and looked premium to match the aesthetic.

Fabrication and Manufacturing

Traditionally, the finish is applied to parts by passing an abrasive disk back and forth on a special mill that is slightly off tram. Tram being thedifference in perpendicularity of the rotating axis and the perpendicular from the part. By doing so, only one side of the abrasive disk makes contact with the part leaving a narrow streak of texture on the part. If the mill were to be perfectly tramed, each subsequent pass would be passing over a surface that has already been abraded. This was my first speed bump I encountered as the machine shop mills were perfectly tramed, and changing the alignment would take many hours to do. I came up with the idea of making passes with a face mill and stepping down each pass from the last by the smallest amount the DRO could resolve. This worked out to be a difference of .0005". This solution was not ideal as one side of the piece would be marginally thinner than the other, and there would be a slighty slope across the piece, but the total difference ammounted to only a few thousandths of an inch which was imperceivable. Center Image


Once I astablished the finish was possible and looked true to form, I began preparing the stock aluminum by cutting and facing all 6 sides to a rough piece slightly larger than the final dimensions. Once this was complete I programmed the mill operations in MasterCAM to then upload onto the machine. The complex nature of the rounded edges and circular cutouts for recessed knobs meant almost nothing was possible by hand and required the aid of a CAM program.

Center Image

The faceplate was designed to fit onto an existing housing for an amplifier, so the mounting hardware had to match the hole pattern and thread type of the case. The shoulder bolts that I found were the wrong dimension and thread type, so they had to be machined down on a lathe. The shoulder length was shortened, and the threads were removed and threaded to an M4. The potentiometers that the knobs mounted to had different length shafts which meant their further mounting on the existing case was limited. This meant I had to account for the difference in mounting by having a negative space on the back side of the faceplate to have the knobs sit on the same plane. You can see the slight cutouts behind the knob cutouts in the MasterCAM figure.

Center Image

The test pieces I made went smoothly when adding the surface finsh, but I ran into my second and largest challenge of the project when trying to add it to the faceplate. The smaller test pieces fit into the vice over its entire length, but the faceplate metal was longer and protruded from the ends of the vice leaving a few inches on each side cantilevered out. Although only a slight amount, the vibrations and flex from the face mill completely changed the characteristics of the passes resulting in an uneven ugly finish. Even when I added material supports below each side, the issue remained. Since I was working on the final piece and was removing material on each pass, I had to be mindful of the total thickness of the part and how the features and alignment would change if I kept making unnecessary passes. I tried horizontal and vertical stripes, but both kept failing which was quite worrying. Eventually I added a second vice to the mill and secured the piece along most of its length which added enough support to keep vibrations and deflexion to a minimum. This along with vertical passes that were shorter were the key to producing the finish I was after with the technique I was using.

Finished surface on the faceplate. Turned out incredible and without noticable surface imperfections. The machining marks in the metal were so small that it gave the faceplate an iridescent look to it at certain viewing angles. Since the faceing paths overlapped and only left the sides of the cut exposed, the light interacts and reflects back differently as you move past it which was an unintentional side effect of the vertical stripes, but it makes the amplifier much more dynamic to look at as it catches and captivates viewers.

All that remained was finishing the outer border of the piece. This includes rounding the corners and adding a champfer to the front edge. Additional MasterCAM code was uploaded and the front surface was protected with a layer of painters tape to protect from chips and other scratches. After proceeding extremely carefully, the finalized faceplate was ready to be installed onto the rest of the amplifier case.

Center Image

The last custom part of the amplifier case that needed to be completed was the back I/O panel which houses all the power, inputs, and some outsputs. I added two XLR outputs to drive external speakers if needed, as well as 4 RCA inputs which is more than enough. I machined out the holes for the connectors from engineering drawings online and located them based off of the PCB they mounted to. No special care needed to be taken to connect the connectors since they mounth through a hole from both sides. No tapping or the such was needed, just a hole of the correct diameter.

Center Image

Final Product

The internal electronic of the amplifier were part of a complete system my dad had sitting around as a project he never got around to. This made assembling the amp much easier as it was just a matter of attatching the various PCBs to the base plate, back I/O, and faceplate, which was made easier by designing the parts to be compatible. Aluminum feet elevated the amp and matched the color fo the faceplate completing the look of the amplifier. As a way of showing off all the hard work spent through the year, the class held an exposition to showcase the wide range of projects people worked on.
