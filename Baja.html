Project Overview

SAE Baja is a competition project hosted by universities around the world that challenges groups of students to design, manufacture, and race off-road vehicles in a series of events to test engineering design skills. At CU Boulder, the team is run as a one-year senior design project consisting of 10-13 students who complete a ground-up design beginning each fall. Over the summer there is a period where the team can gather data from the previous years vehicle to set design goals for improvements in each of the sub-systems.

The year is broken into two sections with the fall semester designated for the design and modeling of the vehicle and the spring semester for manufacturing and testing. The design iterations were worked through and analyzed in CAD software including SolidWorks, Lotus, and ANSYS until they met desired design goals. The drafting was fully completed before the end of the first semester so manufacturing could begin at the start of the spring semester to meet the deadline of competition.

Design Goals

  • Increase top speed from 17 to 25 MPH
  • Greatly improve serviceability and reliability
  • Reduce weight of drivetrain by 30%
  • Demonstrate 4WD/AWD ability
  • Include mounting options for multiple CVT's (continuously variable transmissions)
We used the data collected from the previous years car to set design goals and define metrics for our drivetrain. The goal is to have marginal improvements over previous years cars, so we looked at pain points and performance from the last car to improve upon. First and foremost, the drivetrain's performance can be defined as the vehicles top speed and acceleration, so we aimed to increase the top speed from 17 mph to 25 mph, and to reduce the 100 ft drag time by 1-2 seconds. Secondly, the serviceability of the drivetrain has historically been a challenge because it is so interconnected with all of the other sub-systems. For some components in the drivetrain, most of the rear end of the car had to be removed just to access a single part which was extremely time consuming. Additionally, reducing the weight of the vehicle greatly improves performance because each team is allotted a standard 10 HP engine, so power to weight directly corresponds to accleration and top speed. Our initial goal was to reduce the total system weight of the drivetrain by 30%, but this was later lowered to 15% after further analysis. A new rule that was introduced for the 2022-23 season was a 4WD/AWD requirement, where in the past demonstrating this ability was awarded with extra points in the competition. As a test of 4WD/AWD, all Baja vehicles must be able to pull off of rollers using just the front two wheels. Lastly, our team had access to two CVT's, the CVTech and Gaged CVT. The CVTech has been proven to be reliable in competition with the limitation of a smaller gear range, and the Gaged CVT has a known heat disipation issue but a wider gear range. It was imprtant to performance and testing that the drivetrain could accommidate mounting for both CVT's to better analyze which transmission offered the best performance.

Drivetrain Configurations

In the initial design phase we had two fully specced drivetrain configurations we were considering between. Both had the same initial powertrain from the engine to a cental transfer case, but one configuration had an off-the-shelf lockable rear differential and the other had a custom fully locked rear differential. Both configurations had the option to run different CTV's which gives us more tunability of the drivetrain when testing.

The design advantages of the off-the-shelf configuration were increased reliability and an additional drive mode since the rear differential was switchable. This came at the cost of weight as the complete system was approximately 5 lbs more than the custom configuration. The custom differential was technically more challenging to design, so it would serve as a better learning experience than the other configuration. Both the configurations used the same off-the-shelf front differential which had an underdrive feature we were interested in. This feature allows the front wheels to disconnect when there is ample traction which reduces power loss through the front wheels, increasing the efficency of the powertrain. For the feature to function, the rear differential needed to have a 10-15% smaller gear reduction than the front diff. This creates a difference in output velocities in the front and rear wheels which mechanically disengages the front wheels.

System Integration

The largest integration challenged we faced was the engine placement within the rear chassis of the vehicle. The amount of space was determined by suspension mounting locations, and vehicle wheelbase. This left little space for engine mounting and clearance for servicability. There were two options for changing the engine mounting for the different belt lengths, one was to add vertical spacers under the engine to increase the center-to-center distance to 10", and the other option was to offset the engine backwards until the correct center-to-center was met. Ultimately we decided to pursue the second option of offsetting the engine because it would reduce the lever arm the CVT belt has on the mounting bracket of the chassis.
